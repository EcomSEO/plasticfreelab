# PlasticFreeLab — Competitive Landscape Analysis

**Prepared:** April 2026
**Scope:** Top-10 SERP analysis for Wave 1 priority keywords + broader non-toxic living competitor landscape + 10 concrete positioning moves.
**Target persona:** "Sarah, 34" — evidence-led, Wirecutter-reading, $95–140k HHI, not crunchy-mom (see brand book §2).
**Method:** Live Google US SERPs (April 2026) via WebSearch. Where a site's on-page detail could not be inspected directly (WebFetch restrictions), public structure, known editorial track record, and SERP snippet signal were used to assess strengths/weaknesses. Domain authority estimates are directional, not Moz-exact.

## Part 2 — Broader Competitor Landscape

### Mamavation (mamavation.com)
- **Audience:** Moms, hyper-cautious, PFAS-conscious. Overlaps with PFL's "Amanda" persona more than "Sarah."
- **Strengths:** Actual EPA-certified lab testing — the only competitor commissioning primary organic-fluorine data at scale. Cited by Consumer Reports and NYT.
- **Weaknesses:** Fear-led voice ("TOXIC forever chemicals!"), paywalled raw lab data (MAMAGATE covers ~13% of content), dated design, not ranked-product-oriented. Sarah would bounce off the tone.
- **DA feel:** Mid-50s. Good backlink profile from lab testing.
- **PFL beats them on:** Voice (calm, not alarmed), product ranking clarity (they list good/bad/ugly tiers but rarely a single #1), design, SERP optimization. Also: we can cite them as primary lab source and become the more-readable entry point.

### Gimme the Good Stuff (gimmethegoodstuff.org)
- **Audience:** Parents, pregnancy-forward. Adjacent to Sarah.
- **Strengths:** PhD reviewers, 2010-founded so strong backlink age, "Good/Bad/Sneaky" framework is memorable, good E-E-A-T signals.
- **Weaknesses:** They sell a curated shop — conflict-of-interest baked in. Comparison posts are more categorical than ranked. Slow refresh cadence (several 2024 slugs on 2026 queries). Voice leans toward parent-anxious.
- **DA feel:** High 50s to low 60s.
- **PFL beats them on:** No store conflict, faster refresh, cleaner ranked recommendations, non-parent framing that works for the 40% of Sarah-persona cohorts without kids.

### EcoCult (ecocult.com)
- **Audience:** Urban, fashion-forward, sustainability-minded.
- **Strengths:** Journalist-founded (Alden Wicker), investigative backbone, NYC/Substack pivot, DA ~53.
- **Weaknesses:** Shifted most new content to Substack post-HCU — leaves SERPs open. Fashion-biased; cookware/mattress/water-filter coverage is thin. Not an "actually tested 12 products" operation.
- **PFL beats them on:** Product category breadth (PFL's 5 hubs vs EcoCult's fashion-tilt), ranked comparisons, Google-native rather than Substack-native distribution.

### Going Zero Waste (goingzerowaste.com)
- **Audience:** Zero-waste lifestyle, DIY-inclined.
- **Strengths:** Strong DIY recipes (toothpaste, cleaners), long-tail cluster coverage.
- **Weaknesses:** Almost no product testing, weak comparison content, zero-waste framing drifts away from non-toxic/EDC core. Content can feel repetitive across 8+ years.
- **PFL beats them on:** Investigative depth, ranked product recommendations, EDC/microplastic-first framing vs zero-waste-first.

### The Good Trade (thegoodtrade.com)
- **Audience:** Millennial sustainable-lifestyle women.
- **Strengths:** DA ~70, dominates "best sustainable X" queries via brand age, polished visual design, email list.
- **Weaknesses:** Lifestyle-listicle voice with zero testing. Every "best" post is sponsor-friendly brand disclosure reading. Almost no citations. Relies on aesthetic curation, not evidence. This is the Wirecutter-of-wellness positioning they're faking without doing the work.
- **PFL beats them on:** Every substantive axis — testing, citations, methodology, ranked recommendations. Good Trade's vulnerability is that they rank on brand authority alone.

### The Filtery (thefiltery.com)
- **Audience:** Non-toxic-curious, research-forward, adjacent to Sarah.
- **Strengths:** The closest competitor to PFL's positioning. Good methodology pages, clear voice, non-scolding.
- **Weaknesses:** Small team, slow publishing cadence, no primary lab testing, no house-brand leverage. DA ~35–40.
- **PFL beats them on:** Publishing velocity (5/week vs their ~1/week), primary testing budget, Pinterest distribution, better design system.

### EWG (ewg.org)
- **Audience:** The data source everyone else references.
- **Strengths:** Nonprofit DA in the 80s. Database ownership. Skin Deep and Sunscreen Guide are the indexes of record.
- **Weaknesses:** Activist-coded scoring gets challenged by peer reviewers (2009 SOT survey: 79% said they overstate risks). No ranked comparison output — they list, they don't pick. Site UX is dated.
- **PFL beats them on:** Opinionated #1 picks, Sarah-appropriate voice, comparison structure. Strategy: cite EWG (never attack), aggregate their scores into better-UX tables, own the "EWG verified X, ranked" sub-queries.

### Wirecutter (nytimes.com/wirecutter)
- **Audience:** Mainstream evidence-led buyer. Sarah literally reads Wirecutter.
- **Strengths:** DA 90+, NYT authority, genuine testing, best-in-class methodology pages.
- **Weaknesses:** Admittedly behind on the non-toxic beat — their non-stick recommendations include PFAS-adjacent GenX pans (LeafScore has a widely-cited rebuttal post). Don't cover fluoride-free toothpaste, microplastic-free underwear, or EWG-verified sunscreen as categories. Mattresses: yes. Water filters: yes.
- **PFL beats them on:** Category coverage in non-toxic space, EDC chemistry depth, update cadence on chemical issues. Cannot beat them on head-of-funnel generic terms.

### Good Housekeeping (goodhousekeeping.com)
- **Strengths:** GH Institute testing lab, 140-year brand, DA ~90.
- **Weaknesses:** Content is advertorial-adjacent, slow to cover EDC topics, not PFAS-first in framing, broad lifestyle audience dilutes non-toxic niche.
- **PFL beats them on:** Niche focus, citation density, publishing cadence, calm-nerdy voice over mass-market voice.

### Sustainable Jungle (sustainablejungle.com)
- **Audience:** Gen-Z/young millennial sustainable-living.
- **Strengths:** 10M readers since 2017, broad category coverage, decent DA.
- **Weaknesses:** Heavy affiliate listicles, thin per-product detail, no testing, no primary sources.
- **PFL beats them on:** Testing, citations, ranked specificity.

### Well+Good, Goop, Prevention, Real Simple
- **Strengths:** Massive DAs (85+).
- **Weaknesses:** All cover non-toxic living as a side beat. Goop is tonally anathema to Sarah. Prevention is mass-market health. Real Simple is aspirational-lifestyle. None defend non-toxic queries with the depth of a specialist.
- **PFL beats them on:** Specialization. These are generalists — a niche authority with citation discipline wins niche queries.

### Wedge meta-pattern across all 10
Across every competitor above, exactly *one* (Mamavation) runs primary lab tests, and their lab data is partly paywalled. Nobody else — not EWG, not Wirecutter, not Good Housekeeping, not any of the non-toxic lifestyle blogs — commissions independent chemistry on the products they rank. That's the $30k/year moat PFL can buy.

## Part 3 — Ten Positioning Moves PlasticFreeLab Can Make to Beat This Field

Each move is tied to a specific SERP weakness observed above. Each is executable inside the existing repo/workflow.

### Move 1 — Commission independent lab testing on every comparison post, publish raw PDFs
**Weakness observed:** 9 of 10 cookware results cite Mamavation instead of running their own tests. Nobody in the sunscreen or underwear SERPs has lab data at all.
**Move:** Allocate $25–35k/year to organic-fluorine PFAS screens and textile shed tests at EPA-certified labs (same vendors Mamavation uses). Publish the raw PDF alongside every comparison post under a "Our lab results" header. Link to the PDF from the brief's `sources` frontmatter.
**Why it works:** Google's E-E-A-T framework explicitly rewards primary-source content. Other blogs will start citing *us* (backlinks). The investment pays itself back in 6–9 months via referring-domain growth.

### Move 2 — Tested-vs-researched disclosure label on every product card
**Weakness observed:** Sustainably Kind Living, The Good Trade, One Green Planet all imply testing without clarifying. Sarah can smell this.
**Move:** On every product in every comparison post, render a pill-badge — `TESTED IN HOUSE` / `LAB COMMISSIONED` / `EVALUATED FROM RESEARCH`. Brand book §6.6 already mandates this; make it a UI component, not just copy.
**Why it works:** Reinforces honesty-as-positioning. This is a trust signal Sarah will notice on the first visit, and no competitor has it as a component.

### Move 3 — The "Sarah Test": one-scroll-to-pick rendering
**Weakness observed:** Every comparison post in the top 10 buries the winner 1,500+ words deep. Sarah's explicit frustration (brand book §2) is "4,000-word posts that take 30 minutes to get to the recommendation."
**Move:** Every comparison post opens with a sticky "Our pick" card — product name, price, 40-word why, Buy button — before the H1 scrolls out of view. The long-form research sits below for readers who want it. Include a "Skip to why we picked this" anchor.
**Why it works:** Reduces bounce, directly matches the Wirecutter reading pattern Sarah already uses, and differentiates immediately from The Good Trade / Sustainable Jungle listicle structure.

### Move 4 — Publish the methodology, every time, same URL pattern
**Weakness observed:** Sleep Foundation and Mattress Clarity publish methodology; almost no non-toxic competitor does. Absence of methodology is the single clearest "this is an AI-farmed affiliate page" tell to Google.
**Move:** Every comparison post links to `/methodology/{slug}` — a dedicated page with (a) the selection criteria, (b) how long each product was tested, (c) the lab vendor, (d) conflicts of interest declared, (e) the reviewer. Schema-marked as `CreativeWork` with `reviewAspect`.
**Why it works:** Huge E-E-A-T lift, gives reviewers a concrete rebuttable claim structure, beats every competitor who hand-waves "our experts."

### Move 5 — Named DDS / MD / MS reviewer on each health-adjacent post
**Weakness observed:** NewMouth has a DDS and outranks everyone on fluoride-free toothpaste despite a weaker site overall. Sleep Foundation wins mattresses partly on clinical review signal.
**Move:** Hire one part-time DDS, one part-time RD/nutrition MS, one part-time MPH. Credit them per post via the frontmatter `reviewed_by` field, render their bio inline, and schema-mark each review with `Person` / `MedicalScholarlyArticle` where applicable.
**Why it works:** Concrete, repeatable E-E-A-T advantage on every personal-care and ingestible-adjacent post. Particularly unlocks Hub 3 (Personal Care) which is SERP-dominated by dental/derm content.

### Move 6 — Cite primary literature with PMIDs, not "studies show"
**Weakness observed:** Every non-toxic lifestyle competitor fails the brand book §4 test. "Research shows microplastics may be linked to..." is the SERP norm.
**Move:** Every health claim includes parenthetical citation in the form `(Ragusa et al., 2024, Environment International, n=257, PMID: 39XXXXXX)`. Numbered references at post footer. Use `@cite` MDX shortcode so the brief generator enforces it.
**Why it works:** Sarah trusts specificity. Google rewards cited content post-HCU. Differentiates on every page from scroll-1.

### Move 7 — The "Decoded" interstitial: certifications as a lookup table
**Weakness observed:** No non-toxic mattress top-10 result explains the difference between GOLS, GOTS, OEKO-TEX Standard 100, MADE SAFE, and CertiPUR-US. Readers are asked to trust acronyms.
**Move:** Build one evergreen `/certifications-decoded` page. Each certification gets: what it tests, who audits, which criticisms exist (e.g., CertiPUR-US is industry-funded), a sample certificate PDF. Internal-link from every product card where the cert appears.
**Why it works:** Ranks for every "what does X certification mean" query (long-tail traffic). Reduces bounce rate on comparison posts. Fills a category-wide content gap.

### Move 8 — The "Same as the brand book" visual-test asset: photos on multiple skin tones, with-makeup pilling, water immersion
**Weakness observed:** EWG.org dominates sunscreen queries but provides zero user-experience data. Ao Skincare's #1 spot is thin because they only show one skin tone.
**Move:** For every sunscreen / cosmetic comparison, shoot standardized UX photos: 4 skin tones, pre/post application, 2-hour wear, after sweat. Host on Supabase CDN per the existing skill. Embed as grid on the product card.
**Why it works:** This is a moat. EWG can't do it (they're a database). Wirecutter doesn't want to (not their beat). Goop won't (not their aesthetic). It compounds into Pinterest pins too.

### Move 9 — Aggressive internal linking around "undefended" sub-queries before the head term
**Weakness observed:** Best non-toxic mattresses is a defended SERP PFL can't win from zero. But "which organic mattress brands have been sued" is unclaimed; "is fiberglass in non-toxic mattresses safe" is unclaimed; "Avocado vs Naturepedic latex composition" is unclaimed.
**Move:** For each of the 6 Wave 1 head terms, generate 8–12 cluster posts on the adjacent undefended sub-queries first. Publish them *before* the head-term comparison post. Internal-link all of them up into the comparison.
**Why it works:** Topical authority compounds. Google rewards sites with density before authority on the money page. Every cluster also ranks independently for long-tail volume. The topical map already contains most of these — this move is about sequencing, not new briefs.

### Move 10 — Refresh date stamped visibly, quarterly review cadence enforced by the repo
**Weakness observed:** fulbrightdental.com and noplasticnoproblem.com both rank with 2024-in-slug content. The Good Trade's 2026 posts are thinly updated from 2023. Stale = vulnerable.
**Move:** Every comparison post renders a visible `Last reviewed: April 2026 · Next review: July 2026` strip beneath the H1. Enforce via the `refresh-queue/` folder workflow (CLAUDE.md §Editorial workflow). Each quarterly review re-checks: products still in production, prices still accurate, new entrants, new lab data, new studies. Commit messages record the refresh: `content(plasticfreelab): refresh best-non-toxic-cookware Q2 2026`.
**Why it works:** Freshness is a Google ranking factor for "best X 2026" queries. Stale competitor pages drop. Plus, this ships automatically via the repo-as-editorial-system already specced — no new process.
